# Chapter 4 Overview

This chapter defines the business information and vocabulary for **Housing Community Administration and Resident Communication in Poland**. It gives the dossier a stable set of business concepts so that stakeholders can discuss communities, administrator responsibility, owner participation, communication, and support without drifting into inconsistent language.

## What This Chapter Covers

This chapter explains:

- the main business concepts that structure the domain
- the relationships between communities, administrator companies, owners, and owned estates or units
- the business meaning of community communication, support matters, and durable records
- the terminology choices and semantic ambiguities that later chapters must reuse consistently

## What This Chapter Does Not Cover

This chapter does not define:

- a physical data model, schema, API contract, or message structure
- application permissions, tenancy design, or access-control implementation
- screen behavior or workflow automation
- detailed legal interpretation beyond the business implications already known

## How To Read This Chapter

Read the chapter from framing to detail:

1. `01-information-domain-overview.md` introduces the conceptual landscape.
2. `02-core-business-concepts/` defines the strongest business concepts one by one.
3. `03-relationship-summary.md` highlights the most important concept relationships.
4. `04-glossary.md` provides the consolidated vocabulary reference.
5. `05-semantic-issues-and-open-questions.md` keeps unresolved meaning issues visible.
6. `docs/07-appendices/appendix-c-full-glossary-and-concept-definitions.md` holds extended definitions and notes.

## Relationship To Other Chapters

Chapter 1 frames the domain, stakeholders, and business constraints. Chapter 2 defines the stable business capabilities that operate on the concepts described here. Chapter 3 should then use the vocabulary in this chapter consistently when describing business behavior such as announcements, comments, support tickets, administrator onboarding, assignment, and reassignment.

The intended outcome is simple: this chapter becomes the shared reference for what the main business terms mean before later work turns those meanings into process, architecture, or design.
