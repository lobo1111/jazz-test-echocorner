# Capability View Overview

## Why Capability Mapping Matters Here

EchoCorner sits at the intersection of operator oversight, administrator-company accountability, and owner-facing community interaction. A capability view is useful because it separates the stable business abilities of this domain from the changing ways those abilities may later be performed, organized, or supported.

In this domain, capability mapping is especially important because the same business story must hold together across:

- administrator-company onboarding and oversight
- community assignment and reassignment
- owner eligibility to participate in community communication
- communication and support interactions that must survive organizational change

## Grouping Logic

The capability model is organized into three Level 1 groups:

- **Portfolio governance** for business control over administrator-company participation and community responsibility
- **Community participation management** for owner entitlement, announcements, and permitted discussion
- **Service accountability management** for support handling and the records that preserve continuity

These groups keep strategic governance, day-to-day participation, and accountability concerns distinct without breaking the domain into an overly detailed taxonomy.

## Naming And Decomposition Principles

The chapter uses the following rules when naming capabilities:

- use durable business language rather than application or workflow language
- name the business ability, not the person, team, or screen that performs it
- keep the level of detail low enough that Chapter 3 can reuse the names directly
- introduce deeper decomposition only where the domain would otherwise hide materially different business responsibilities

For that reason, the chapter keeps the main map at Level 1 and Level 2. Level 3 detail is deferred unless later work proves that a capability is too broad for business reasoning.

## Capability Versus Process Versus Role

A **capability** states what the business must be able to do, such as **Community assignment governance** or **Support ticket management**.

A **process** describes how work unfolds over time, such as how a reassignment is initiated, approved, and completed. Those time-based flows belong mainly in Chapter 3.

A **role** identifies who is accountable or involved, such as the **Operator**, **Administrator company**, or **Owner**. Roles consume or perform capabilities, but roles are not capabilities themselves.

## Modeling Stance For This Dossier

This capability view assumes that the following business invariants remain stable:

- each community has exactly one active administrator company at a time
- historical administrator assignments remain part of the business record
- an owner may participate in multiple communities through owned estates or units
- community communication rights depend on business eligibility, not public access

Those invariants make governance, entitlement, and record stewardship central capabilities rather than optional supporting concerns.
