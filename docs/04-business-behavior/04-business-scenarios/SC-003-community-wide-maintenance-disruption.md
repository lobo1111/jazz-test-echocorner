# SC-003: Community-Wide Maintenance Disruption

## Business Context

A disruptive event affects many owners in one **Community** at the same time. The situation creates pressure on official communication, owner discussion, and support handling under the responsibility of the active **Administrator company**.

## Triggering Event

A significant maintenance or service disruption emerges and quickly becomes relevant to a large share of the community.

## Narrative

The responsible **Administrator company** first needs to make the community aware of the disruption through an official communication. Once that occurs, owners may seek clarification, react to the announcement, or raise direct support matters about their own circumstances. What begins as one community-level event can therefore produce several kinds of business behavior at once.

The central business question is how to keep the disruption understandable and accountable without letting the response collapse into confusion. Some owner interactions may belong in the governed discussion around the announcement. Others may need separate **Support tickets** because they concern specific harm, urgency, or estate- or unit-level effects. The business must also decide when many similar matters should still be treated as one shared disruption and when they justify distinct accountable handling.

This scenario shows how communication and support are linked but not identical. A community-wide disruption can require both a shared public business response and separate owner-specific follow-up.

## Key Decisions

- When is one official announcement enough, and when are separate accountable support matters also needed?
- Which owner contributions remain part of announcement discussion, and which should become **Support tickets**?
- Should a reported effect be understood as community-wide, estate-specific, or unit-specific?
- How should the **Administrator company** preserve clarity when many owners are affected at once?

## Possible Outcomes

- The disruption is handled through clear official communication plus accountable treatment of the owner matters that require separate follow-up.
- The disruption stays mostly at community level, and only a small number of owner-specific exceptions require separate support handling.
- The response becomes fragmented because the business boundary between announcement, comment, and ticket handling is not applied consistently.

## Risks, Tensions, Or Observations

- Volume pressure can expose whether the domain has drawn a clear enough line between communication and support.
- Duplicate owner reports may create confusion if they are neither grouped nor clearly tied back to the broader disruption context.
- The scenario highlights the unresolved question of whether support scope should be decided mainly at community level or at estate- or unit-level depending on the case.
